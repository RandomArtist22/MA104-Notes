MA 104: Ordinary Differential Equations - Lecture 6

Related: MA 104 Course Overview | Lecture 5 - MA104 | Lecture 7 - MA104

Recall: Second-Order Homogeneous Linear ODE

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{1} $$ on interval $I$.
  • $y1, y2$ are linearly independent (L.I.) solutions on $I$.
  • Any other solution $y$ on $I$ can be written as
$$ y(x) = c1 y1(x) + c2 y2(x) $$ for constants $c1, c2$.

At any point $x0 \in I$: $$ y(x0) = c1 y1(x0) + c2 y2(x0) $$ $$ y'(x0) = c1 y1'(x0) + c2 y2'(x0) $$

This linear system has unique solution $(c1,c2)$ if and only if $$ W(y1,y2)(x0) = y1(x0)y2'(x0) - y1'(x0)y2(x0) \neq 0 $$

Wronskian

Definition: For two twice-differentiable functions $y1, y2$ on $I$: $$ W(y1,y2)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y1(x) & y2(x) \\ y1'(x) & y2'(x) \end{vmatrix} = y1 y2' - y1' y2 $$
  • $W: I \to \mathbb{R}$ is a function (or map).
Example: $y1 = e^x$, $y2 = e^{2x}$ $W(e^x, e^{2x}) = e^x \cdot 2e^{2x} - e^{2x} \cdot e^x = e^{3x} \neq 0$ everywhere.

Wronskian and Linear Independence

Theorem: If $y1, y2$ are L.I. and differentiable on $I = (a,b)$, then $$ W(y1,y2)(x) = 0 \quad \forall x \in I $$ Proof (contrapositive style): Assume $c1 y1 + c2 y2 \equiv 0$ with not both $ci=0$. Differentiate ‚Üí system $$ \begin{pmatrix} y1 & y2 \\ y1' & y2' \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c1 \\ c2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$ Determinant $W=0$. Converse is NOT always true (counterexample): $y1(x)=x^2$, $y2(x)=x|x|$ on $\mathbb{R}$. $W \equiv 0$ everywhere, but they are L.I. (no constants satisfy $c1 x^2 + c2 x|x| \equiv 0$). However: For solutions of the same linear homogeneous ODE (1) with continuous $P,Q$, the converse is true.

Abel‚Äôs Theorem (Vanishing Property)

Theorem (Abel): If $y1, y2$ solve (1) and $P,Q$ continuous on $I$, then $$ W(y1,y2)(x) = c \exp\left( -\int P(x)\, dx \right) $$ where $c$ is constant (depends on choice of $y1,y2$, not on $x$). Remark: Either $W \equiv 0$ on $I$ or $W(x) \neq 0$ for all $x \in I$. Proof outline: Differentiate Wronskian: $$ W' = y1 y2'' - y1'' y2 = -P(y1 y2' - y1' y2) = -P W $$ $$ \frac{W'}{W} = -P \implies W(x) = c e^{-\int P\, dx} $$ Consequences:
  • To check L.I., evaluate $W$ at one convenient point $x0 \in I$.
  • If $\{y1,y2\}$ and $\{y1^,y2^\}$ are two fundamental sets, then $W(y1,y2) = k \cdot W(y1^,y2^)$ (multiplicative constant).
Example: $y'' - \frac{4}{x} y' + \frac{6}{x^2} y = 0$ $y1=x^2$, $y2=x^3$ are L.I. solutions. $W(x^2,x^3) = x^2 \cdot 3x^2 - x^3 \cdot 2x = x^4 \neq 0$ for $x \neq 0$. At $x=0$, $W(0)=0$, but solutions are still L.I. on $(0,\infty)$ or $(-\infty,0)$.

Nonhomogeneous Linear ODEs

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = R(x) \tag{3} $$

General solution on interval $I$: $$ y(x) = yh(x) + yp(x) $$
  • $yh = c1 y1 + c2 y2$ = general solution of homogeneous (1).
  • $yp$ = any particular solution of (3) (no arbitrary constants).

Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Suitable when $R(x)$ is exponential, polynomial, sine/cosine, or product/sum of these (and coefficients $P,Q$ constant).

Table of guesses (from Kreyszig):
Term in $R(x)$Choice for $yp(x)$
$k e^{\alpha x}$$C e^{\alpha x}$
$k x^n$ ($n=0,1,\dots$)$Kn x^n + \dots + K1 x + K0$
$k \cos \omega x$ or $k \sin \omega x$$K \cos \omega x + M \sin \omega x$
$k e^{\alpha x} \cos \omega x$ or sin$e^{\alpha x}(K \cos \omega x + M \sin \omega x)$
Rules:
  • Basic Rule: Match form, plug in, solve for coefficients.
  • Modification Rule: If guess overlaps with homogeneous solution, multiply by $x$ (or $x^2$ for double root).
  • Sum Rule: For sum, take sum of individual guesses.
Example (modification): $y'' + 3y' + 2.25y = -10 e^{-1.5x}$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=0$ Homogeneous: roots $-1.5$ (double) ‚Üí $yh = (c1 + c2 x)e^{-1.5x}$ Guess $yp = C x^2 e^{-1.5x}$ (multiply by $x^2$). Substitute ‚Üí $C = -5$. Particular: $yp = -5 x^2 e^{-1.5x}$.

Annihilator Method (Advanced Undetermined Coefficients)

Annihilator of $f$: Differential operator $A$ such that $A[f] = 0$. Examples:
  • $f = e^{mx}$ ‚Üí $A = D - m$
  • $f = x^n$ ‚Üí $A = D^{n+1}$
  • $f = \sin(ax+b)$ ‚Üí $A = D^2 + a^2$
  • $f = x^n e^{mx}$ ‚Üí $A = (D-m)^{n+1}$
Procedure for $L[y] = R(x)$ (constant coeff $L$):
  1. Find annihilator $A$ with $A[R]=0$.
  2. Solve higher-order homogeneous $(A L)[y] = 0$.
  3. General solution of $(A L)y=0$ = $yh +$ extra terms.
  4. Take $yp$ = linear combination of extra terms only (those not in kernel of $L$).
  5. Plug into original equation to find constants.
Example: $y'' + y = x^2 + x$ $L = D^2 + 1$ $A = D^3$ (kills $x^2+x$) $(D^3)(D^2+1)y = 0$ ‚Üí characteristic $r^5 + r^3 = r^3(r^2+1)=0$ Roots: $0$ (multiplicity 3), $\pm i$ General: $y = c1 + c2 x + c3 x^2 + c4 \cos x + c5 \sin x$ $yh = d1 \cos x + d2 \sin x$ Thus $yp = c1 + c2 x + c3 x^2$ Substitute ‚Üí $c3=1$, $c2=1$, $c1=-2$ $yp = x^2 + x - 2$

Summary of Lecture 6

  • Wronskian detects linear independence for solutions of linear homogeneous ODEs.
  • Abel‚Äôs theorem: $W(x) = c \exp(-\int P\, dx)$ ‚Üí either always zero or never zero.
  • Nonhomogeneous general solution = $yh + yp$.
  • Undetermined coefficients: Quick algebraic method for special $R(x)$.
  • Annihilator: Systematic way to guess form of $yp$ by solving higher-order homogeneous equation.
