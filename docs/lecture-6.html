MA 104: Ordinary Differential Equations - Lecture 6

Related: MA 104 Course Overview | Lecture 5 - MA104 | Lecture 7 - MA104

Recall: Second-Order Homogeneous Linear ODE

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{1} $$

on interval $I$.

  • $y_1, y_2$ are linearly independent (L.I.) solutions on $I$.
  • Any other solution $y$ on $I$ can be written as
$$ y(x) = c_1 y_1(x) + c_2 y_2(x) $$

for constants $c_1, c_2$.

At any point $x_0 \in I$:

$$ y(x_0) = c_1 y_1(x_0) + c_2 y_2(x_0) $$ $$ y'(x_0) = c_1 y_1'(x_0) + c_2 y_2'(x_0) $$

This linear system has unique solution $(c_1,c_2)$ if and only if

$$ W(y_1,y_2)(x_0) = y_1(x_0)y_2'(x_0) - y_1'(x_0)y_2(x_0) \neq 0 $$

Wronskian

Definition: For two twice-differentiable functions $y_1, y_2$ on $I$:

$$ W(y_1,y_2)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1(x) & y_2(x) \\ y_1'(x) & y_2'(x) \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2 $$
  • $W: I \to \mathbb{R}$ is a function (or map).

Example: $y_1 = e^x$, $y_2 = e^{2x}$
$W(e^x, e^{2x}) = e^x \cdot 2e^{2x} - e^{2x} \cdot e^x = e^{3x} \neq 0$ everywhere.

Wronskian and Linear Independence

Theorem:
If $y_1, y_2$ are L.I. and differentiable on $I = (a,b)$, then

$$ W(y_1,y_2)(x) = 0 \quad \forall x \in I $$

Proof (contrapositive style):
Assume $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \equiv 0$ with not both $c_i=0$. Differentiate ‚Üí system

$$ \begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Determinant $W=0$.

Converse is NOT always true (counterexample): $y_1(x)=x^2$, $y_2(x)=x|x|$ on $\mathbb{R}$.
$W \equiv 0$ everywhere, but they are L.I. (no constants satisfy $c_1 x^2 + c_2 x|x| \equiv 0$).

However: For solutions of the same linear homogeneous ODE (1) with continuous $P,Q$, the converse is true.

Abel‚Äôs Theorem (Vanishing Property)

Theorem (Abel):
If $y_1, y_2$ solve (1) and $P,Q$ continuous on $I$, then

$$ W(y_1,y_2)(x) = c \exp\left( -\int P(x)\, dx \right) $$

where $c$ is constant (depends on choice of $y_1,y_2$, not on $x$).

Remark:
Either $W \equiv 0$ on $I$ or $W(x) \neq 0$ for all $x \in I$.

Proof outline: Differentiate Wronskian:

$$ W' = y_1 y_2'' - y_1'' y_2 = -P(y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2) = -P W $$ $$ \frac{W'}{W} = -P \implies W(x) = c e^{-\int P\, dx} $$

Consequences:

  • To check L.I., evaluate $W$ at one convenient point $x_0 \in I$.
  • If $\{y_1,y_2\}$ and $\{y_1^*,y_2^*\}$ are two fundamental sets, then $W(y_1,y_2) = k \cdot W(y_1^*,y_2^*)$ (multiplicative constant).

Example: $y'' - \frac{4}{x} y' + \frac{6}{x^2} y = 0$
$y_1=x^2$, $y_2=x^3$ are L.I. solutions.
$W(x^2,x^3) = x^2 \cdot 3x^2 - x^3 \cdot 2x = x^4 \neq 0$ for $x \neq 0$.
At $x=0$, $W(0)=0$, but solutions are still L.I. on $(0,\infty)$ or $(-\infty,0)$.

Nonhomogeneous Linear ODEs

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = R(x) \tag{3} $$

General solution on interval $I$:

$$ y(x) = y_h(x) + y_p(x) $$
  • $y_h = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ = general solution of homogeneous (1).
  • $y_p$ = any particular solution of (3) (no arbitrary constants).

Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Suitable when $R(x)$ is exponential, polynomial, sine/cosine, or product/sum of these (and coefficients $P,Q$ constant).

Table of guesses (from Kreyszig):

Term in $R(x)$Choice for $y_p(x)$
$k e^{\alpha x}$$C e^{\alpha x}$
$k x^n$ ($n=0,1,\dots$)$K_n x^n + \dots + K_1 x + K_0$
$k \cos \omega x$ or $k \sin \omega x$$K \cos \omega x + M \sin \omega x$
$k e^{\alpha x} \cos \omega x$ or sin$e^{\alpha x}(K \cos \omega x + M \sin \omega x)$

Rules:

  • Basic Rule: Match form, plug in, solve for coefficients.
  • Modification Rule: If guess overlaps with homogeneous solution, multiply by $x$ (or $x^2$ for double root).
  • Sum Rule: For sum, take sum of individual guesses.

Example (modification): $y'' + 3y' + 2.25y = -10 e^{-1.5x}$, $y(0)=1$, $y'(0)=0$
Homogeneous: roots $-1.5$ (double) ‚Üí $y_h = (c_1 + c_2 x)e^{-1.5x}$
Guess $y_p = C x^2 e^{-1.5x}$ (multiply by $x^2$).
Substitute ‚Üí $C = -5$.
Particular: $y_p = -5 x^2 e^{-1.5x}$.

Annihilator Method (Advanced Undetermined Coefficients)

Annihilator of $f$: Differential operator $A$ such that $A[f] = 0$.

Examples:

  • $f = e^{mx}$ ‚Üí $A = D - m$
  • $f = x^n$ ‚Üí $A = D^{n+1}$
  • $f = \sin(ax+b)$ ‚Üí $A = D^2 + a^2$
  • $f = x^n e^{mx}$ ‚Üí $A = (D-m)^{n+1}$

Procedure for $L[y] = R(x)$ (constant coeff $L$):

  1. Find annihilator $A$ with $A[R]=0$.
  2. Solve higher-order homogeneous $(A L)[y] = 0$.
  3. General solution of $(A L)y=0$ = $y_h +$ extra terms.
  4. Take $y_p$ = linear combination of extra terms only (those not in kernel of $L$).
  5. Plug into original equation to find constants.

Example: $y'' + y = x^2 + x$
$L = D^2 + 1$
$A = D^3$ (kills $x^2+x$)
$(D^3)(D^2+1)y = 0$ ‚Üí characteristic $r^5 + r^3 = r^3(r^2+1)=0$
Roots: $0$ (multiplicity 3), $\pm i$
General: $y = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2 + c_4 \cos x + c_5 \sin x$
$y_h = d_1 \cos x + d_2 \sin x$
Thus $y_p = c_1 + c_2 x + c_3 x^2$
Substitute ‚Üí $c_3=1$, $c_2=1$, $c_1=-2$
$y_p = x^2 + x - 2$

Summary of Lecture 6

  • Wronskian detects linear independence for solutions of linear homogeneous ODEs.
  • Abel‚Äôs theorem: $W(x) = c \exp(-\int P\, dx)$ ‚Üí either always zero or never zero.
  • Nonhomogeneous general solution = $y_h + y_p$.
  • Undetermined coefficients: Quick algebraic method for special $R(x)$.
  • Annihilator: Systematic way to guess form of $y_p$ by solving higher-order homogeneous equation.
