Second-Order Linear ODEs

Explicit form: $$ y'' = f(x, y, y') $$ Linear if $f$ is linear in $y$ and $y'$: $$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = R(x) \tag{1} $$
  • $P,Q,R$ are functions of $x$ only.
  • Homogeneous if $R(x) \equiv 0$:
$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{2} $$
  • Nonhomogeneous otherwise.
Key Properties:
  • Equation (1) is linear in $y$ and its derivatives.
  • $y \equiv 0$ is always a solution of the homogeneous equation (2).
  • If $yh$ is the general solution of (2) and $yp$ is any particular solution of (1), then
$$ y = yh + yp $$ is the general solution of (1).

Similarity with First-Order Linear ODEs

A general second-order ODE is $y'' = f(x,y,y')$. It becomes linear precisely when $f(x,y,y') = R(x) - P(x)y - Q(x)y'$.

Superposition Principle (Homogeneous Case)

Theorem: If $y1(x)$ and $y2(x)$ are solutions of (2) on an interval $I$, then for any constants $c1, c2$, $$ y = c1 y1 + c2 y2 $$ is also a solution of (2). Proof (direct substitution): $$ (c1 y1 + c2 y2)'' + P(c1 y1 + c2 y2)' + Q(c1 y1 + c2 y2) = c1(y1'' + P y1' + Q y1) + c2(y2'' + P y2' + Q y2) = 0 $$ Example: $y'' + y = 0$ $y1 = \cos x$, $y2 = \sin x$ are solutions. Any $c1 \cos x + c2 \sin x$ is also a solution.

General Solution & Basis

General solution of homogeneous (2) on open interval $I$: $$ y(x) = c1 y1(x) + c2 y2(x) $$ where $y1, y2$ are linearly independent (not scalar multiples of each other) solutions on $I$. $y1, y2$ form a basis (or fundamental set) of solutions. Linearly independent: $c1 y1 + c2 y2 \equiv 0$ implies $c1 = c2 = 0$.

Initial Value Problem (IVP)

For homogeneous (2): $$ y(x0) = y0, \quad y'(x0) = y1 $$ Two conditions determine $c1, c2$ uniquely.

Existence & Uniqueness Theorem (2nd-Order Linear)

Theorem: If $P(x), Q(x)$ are continuous on open interval $I$ containing $x0$, then the IVP $$ y'' + P y' + Q y = R(x), \quad y(x0)=y0,\ y'(x0)=y1 $$ has a unique solution on the entire interval $I$. Operator viewpoint: Define linear operator $L[y] = y'' + P y' + Q y$. Null space $N(L) = \{y \in C^2(I) : L[y]=0\}$ is a 2-dimensional vector space. Proof sketch:
  1. Produce two linearly independent solutions $y1, y2$ by solving special IVPs:
  • $y1$: $y(x0)=1$, $y'(x0)=0$
  • $y2$: $y(x0)=0$, $y'(x0)=1$
2. Any solution $y$ satisfies the same IVP as $c1 y1 + c2 y2$ with $c1=y(x0)$, $c2=y'(x0)$ ‚Üí uniqueness.

Reduction of Order (When One Solution is Known)

Given homogeneous (2) and one known nontrivial solution $y1(x)$ on $I$. Seek second solution of form $y2 = u(x) y1(x)$.

Substitute: $$ y2' = u' y1 + u y1', \quad y2'' = u'' y1 + 2 u' y1' + u y1'' $$ Plug into (2) ‚Üí after simplification (using $L[y1]=0$): $$ u'' y1 + u' (2 y1' + P y1) = 0 $$ Let $v = u'$: $$ v' + \left( \frac{2 y1'}{y1} + P \right) v = 0 $$ First-order separable: $$ \frac{dv}{v} = -\left( \frac{2 y1'}{y1} + P \right) dx $$ $$ \ln |v| = -2 \ln |y1| - \int P\, dx + C $$ $$ v = \frac{C}{y1^2} e^{-\int P\, dx} $$ Take $C=1$: $$ u = \int \frac{1}{y1^2} e^{-\int P\, dx}\, dx $$ $$ y2 = y1 \int \frac{e^{-\int P\, dx}}{y1^2}\, dx $$

Example: $x^2 y'' + x y' - y = 0$ (or $y'' + \frac{1}{x} y' - \frac{1}{x^2} y = 0$) Known: $y1 = x$ $P = 1/x$ $$ u = \int \frac{1}{x^2} e^{-\int dx/x}\, dx = \int \frac{1}{x^2} \cdot \frac{1}{x}\, dx = \int x^{-3}\, dx = -\frac{1}{2x^2} $$ $$ y2 = x \left( -\frac{1}{2x^2} \right) = -\frac{1}{2x} $$ General solution: $y = c1 x - \frac{c2}{2x}$

Homogeneous Linear ODE with Constant Coefficients

$$ y'' + p y' + q y = 0 \tag{3} \quad (p,q \text{ constants}) $$

Characteristic equation: $$ r^2 + p r + q = 0 $$ Roots: $$ r = \frac{-p \pm \sqrt{p^2 - 4q}}{2} $$

Case I: Two distinct real roots $r1 \neq r2$

Basis: $\{e^{r1 x}, e^{r2 x}\}$ General solution: $$ y = c1 e^{r1 x} + c2 e^{r2 x} $$ Example: $y'' + 6y' + 5y = 0$ $r^2 + 6r + 5 = 0 \implies (r+5)(r+1)=0 \implies r=-5,-1$ $y = c1 e^{-5x} + c2 e^{-x}$

Case II: Repeated real root $r = -p/2$

Basis: $\{e^{r x}, x e^{r x}\}$ General solution: $$ y = (c1 + c2 x) e^{r x} $$ Example: $y'' + 6y' + 9y = 0$, $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=1$ $r = -3$ (double) $y = (c1 + c2 x) e^{-3x}$ $y(0)=c1=3$ $y' = -3(c1 + c2 x)e^{-3x} + c2 e^{-3x}$ ‚Üí $y'(0) = -3c1 + c2 =1 \implies c2=10$ $y = (3 + 10x) e^{-3x}$

Case III: Complex conjugate roots $\alpha \pm i\beta$

Basis: $\{e^{\alpha x} \cos \beta x, e^{\alpha x} \sin \beta x\}$ General solution: $$ y = e^{\alpha x} (c1 \cos \beta x + c2 \sin \beta x) $$ Example: $y'' + 4y' + 13y = 0$, $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=1$ $r^2 + 4r + 13=0 \implies r = -2 \pm 3i$ $y = e^{-2x} (c1 \cos 3x + c2 \sin 3x)$ Apply ICs ‚Üí $c1=3$, $c2=7/3$

Euler-Cauchy (Equidimensional) Equations

Form: $$ x^2 y'' + a x y' + b y = 0 \tag{4} \quad (a,b \text{ constants, } x>0 \text{ or } x<0) $$

Substitution $y = x^m$: $$ m(m-1) + a m + b = 0 \quad \text{(auxiliary equation)} $$ Solutions:
  • Distinct real $m1 \neq m2$: $y = c1 x^{m1} + c2 x^{m2}$
  • Repeated $m$: $y = (c1 + c2 \ln |x|) x^m$
  • Complex $m = \alpha \pm i\beta$: $y = |x|^\alpha [c1 \cos(\beta \ln |x|) + c2 \sin(\beta \ln |x|)]$

Summary of Lecture 5

  • Linear second-order ODEs: standard form, homogeneous vs nonhomogeneous.
  • Superposition ‚Üí general solution = $c1 y1 + c2 y2$ (linearly independent basis).
  • Existence/uniqueness: guaranteed on intervals where $P,Q$ continuous.
  • Reduction of order: powerful when one solution known.
  • Constant coefficients ‚Üí characteristic polynomial (3 cases).
  • Euler-Cauchy ‚Üí power substitution, auxiliary quadratic in $m$.
