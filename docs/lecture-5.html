Second-Order Linear ODEs

Explicit form:

$$ y'' = f(x, y, y') $$

Linear if $f$ is linear in $y$ and $y'$:

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = R(x) \tag{1} $$
  • $P,Q,R$ are functions of $x$ only.
  • Homogeneous if $R(x) \equiv 0$:
$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{2} $$
  • Nonhomogeneous otherwise.

Key Properties:

  • Equation (1) is linear in $y$ and its derivatives.
  • $y \equiv 0$ is always a solution of the homogeneous equation (2).
  • If $y_h$ is the general solution of (2) and $y_p$ is any particular solution of (1), then
$$ y = y_h + y_p $$

is the general solution of (1).

Similarity with First-Order Linear ODEs

A general second-order ODE is $y'' = f(x,y,y')$.
It becomes linear precisely when $f(x,y,y') = R(x) - P(x)y - Q(x)y'$.

Superposition Principle (Homogeneous Case)

Theorem: If $y_1(x)$ and $y_2(x)$ are solutions of (2) on an interval $I$, then for any constants $c_1, c_2$,

$$ y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 $$

is also a solution of (2).

Proof (direct substitution):

$$ (c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2)'' + P(c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2)' + Q(c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2) = c_1(y_1'' + P y_1' + Q y_1) + c_2(y_2'' + P y_2' + Q y_2) = 0 $$

Example: $y'' + y = 0$
$y_1 = \cos x$, $y_2 = \sin x$ are solutions.
Any $c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x$ is also a solution.

General Solution & Basis

General solution of homogeneous (2) on open interval $I$:

$$ y(x) = c_1 y_1(x) + c_2 y_2(x) $$

where $y_1, y_2$ are linearly independent (not scalar multiples of each other) solutions on $I$.
$y_1, y_2$ form a basis (or fundamental set) of solutions.

Linearly independent: $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 \equiv 0$ implies $c_1 = c_2 = 0$.

Initial Value Problem (IVP)

For homogeneous (2):

$$ y(x_0) = y_0, \quad y'(x_0) = y_1 $$

Two conditions determine $c_1, c_2$ uniquely.

Existence & Uniqueness Theorem (2nd-Order Linear)

Theorem:
If $P(x), Q(x)$ are continuous on open interval $I$ containing $x_0$, then the IVP

$$ y'' + P y' + Q y = R(x), \quad y(x_0)=y_0,\ y'(x_0)=y_1 $$

has a unique solution on the entire interval $I$.

Operator viewpoint: Define linear operator $L[y] = y'' + P y' + Q y$.
Null space $N(L) = \{y \in C^2(I) : L[y]=0\}$ is a 2-dimensional vector space.

Proof sketch:

  1. Produce two linearly independent solutions $y_1, y_2$ by solving special IVPs:
  • $y_1$: $y(x_0)=1$, $y'(x_0)=0$
  • $y_2$: $y(x_0)=0$, $y'(x_0)=1$
  1. Any solution $y$ satisfies the same IVP as $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ with $c_1=y(x_0)$, $c_2=y'(x_0)$ ‚Üí uniqueness.

Reduction of Order (When One Solution is Known)

Given homogeneous (2) and one known nontrivial solution $y_1(x)$ on $I$.
Seek second solution of form $y_2 = u(x) y_1(x)$.

Substitute:

$$ y_2' = u' y_1 + u y_1', \quad y_2'' = u'' y_1 + 2 u' y_1' + u y_1'' $$

Plug into (2) ‚Üí after simplification (using $L[y_1]=0$):

$$ u'' y_1 + u' (2 y_1' + P y_1) = 0 $$

Let $v = u'$:

$$ v' + \left( \frac{2 y_1'}{y_1} + P \right) v = 0 $$

First-order separable:

$$ \frac{dv}{v} = -\left( \frac{2 y_1'}{y_1} + P \right) dx $$ $$ \ln |v| = -2 \ln |y_1| - \int P\, dx + C $$ $$ v = \frac{C}{y_1^2} e^{-\int P\, dx} $$

Take $C=1$:

$$ u = \int \frac{1}{y_1^2} e^{-\int P\, dx}\, dx $$ $$ y_2 = y_1 \int \frac{e^{-\int P\, dx}}{y_1^2}\, dx $$

Example: $x^2 y'' + x y' - y = 0$ (or $y'' + \frac{1}{x} y' - \frac{1}{x^2} y = 0$)
Known: $y_1 = x$
$P = 1/x$

$$ u = \int \frac{1}{x^2} e^{-\int dx/x}\, dx = \int \frac{1}{x^2} \cdot \frac{1}{x}\, dx = \int x^{-3}\, dx = -\frac{1}{2x^2} $$ $$ y_2 = x \left( -\frac{1}{2x^2} \right) = -\frac{1}{2x} $$

General solution: $y = c_1 x - \frac{c_2}{2x}$

Homogeneous Linear ODE with Constant Coefficients

$$ y'' + p y' + q y = 0 \tag{3} \quad (p,q \text{ constants}) $$

Characteristic equation:

$$ r^2 + p r + q = 0 $$

Roots:

$$ r = \frac{-p \pm \sqrt{p^2 - 4q}}{2} $$

Case I: Two distinct real roots $r_1 \neq r_2$

Basis: $\{e^{r_1 x}, e^{r_2 x}\}$
General solution:

$$ y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x} $$

Example: $y'' + 6y' + 5y = 0$
$r^2 + 6r + 5 = 0 \implies (r+5)(r+1)=0 \implies r=-5,-1$
$y = c_1 e^{-5x} + c_2 e^{-x}$

Case II: Repeated real root $r = -p/2$

Basis: $\{e^{r x}, x e^{r x}\}$
General solution:

$$ y = (c_1 + c_2 x) e^{r x} $$

Example: $y'' + 6y' + 9y = 0$, $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=1$
$r = -3$ (double)
$y = (c_1 + c_2 x) e^{-3x}$
$y(0)=c_1=3$
$y' = -3(c_1 + c_2 x)e^{-3x} + c_2 e^{-3x}$ ‚Üí $y'(0) = -3c_1 + c_2 =1 \implies c_2=10$
$y = (3 + 10x) e^{-3x}$

Case III: Complex conjugate roots $\alpha \pm i\beta$

Basis: $\{e^{\alpha x} \cos \beta x, e^{\alpha x} \sin \beta x\}$
General solution:

$$ y = e^{\alpha x} (c_1 \cos \beta x + c_2 \sin \beta x) $$

Example: $y'' + 4y' + 13y = 0$, $y(0)=3$, $y'(0)=1$
$r^2 + 4r + 13=0 \implies r = -2 \pm 3i$
$y = e^{-2x} (c_1 \cos 3x + c_2 \sin 3x)$
Apply ICs ‚Üí $c_1=3$, $c_2=7/3$

Euler-Cauchy (Equidimensional) Equations

Form:

$$ x^2 y'' + a x y' + b y = 0 \tag{4} \quad (a,b \text{ constants, } x>0 \text{ or } x<0) $$

Substitution $y = x^m$:

$$ m(m-1) + a m + b = 0 \quad \text{(auxiliary equation)} $$

Solutions:

  • Distinct real $m_1 \neq m_2$: $y = c_1 x^{m_1} + c_2 x^{m_2}$
  • Repeated $m$: $y = (c_1 + c_2 \ln |x|) x^m$
  • Complex $m = \alpha \pm i\beta$: $y = |x|^\alpha [c_1 \cos(\beta \ln |x|) + c_2 \sin(\beta \ln |x|)]$

Summary of Lecture 5

  • Linear second-order ODEs: standard form, homogeneous vs nonhomogeneous.
  • Superposition ‚Üí general solution = $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ (linearly independent basis).
  • Existence/uniqueness: guaranteed on intervals where $P,Q$ continuous.
  • Reduction of order: powerful when one solution known.
  • Constant coefficients ‚Üí characteristic polynomial (3 cases).
  • Euler-Cauchy ‚Üí power substitution, auxiliary quadratic in $m$.
