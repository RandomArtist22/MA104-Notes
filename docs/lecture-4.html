Review: Existence & Uniqueness Theorem

Standard IVP:

$$ y' = f(x,y), \quad y(x_0) = y_0 \tag{1} $$

Coordinate Shift (to simplify proof): Let $\tilde{x} = x - x_0$, $\tilde{y} = y - y_0$.
New IVP (at origin):

$$ \tilde{y}' = f(\tilde{x} + x_0, \tilde{y} + y_0), \quad \tilde{y}(0) = 0 \tag{2} $$

Theorem (Picard‚ÄìLindel√∂f): If $f(x,y)$ and $\partial f/\partial y$ are continuous in rectangle
$D: |x| < a$, $|y| < b$,
then there exists $\alpha = \min\{a, b/K\}$ (where $|f| \leq K$ in $D$) such that (1) has a unique solution $y = \phi(x)$ on $|x - x_0| < \alpha$.

Geometric Interpretation (rectangle $D$):

  • $f$ must be bounded ($|f| \leq K$) inside $D$.
  • Solution stays inside $D$ for $|x| < \alpha$.
  • Outside $D$ ‚Üí no guarantee.
  • Solution must be continuous (since differentiable).

Example:

$$ y' = 1 + y^2, \quad y(0) = 0 $$

Choose $D: |x| < 5$, $|y| < 3$.
$K = 1 + 9 = 10$, $\alpha = \min\{5, 3/10\} = 0.3$.
‚Üí Unique solution exists on $|x| < 0.3$.
(Global solution $y = \tan x$ exists but not valid in larger intervals crossing discontinuities.)

Integral Equation Formulation (Equivalent to IVP)

Assume $\phi(x)$ solves (1) with $\phi(0)=0$.
Then:

$$ \phi'(x) = f(x, \phi(x)), \quad \phi(0)=0 $$

Integrate:

$$ \phi(x) = \int_0^x f(t, \phi(t))\, dt \tag{3} $$

Equivalence:

  • Any $C^1$ solution of (1) satisfies (3).
  • Any continuous solution of (3) is differentiable and satisfies (1) (by Fundamental Theorem of Calculus).

Advantage: (3) is easier to prove has unique solution ‚Üí implies same for IVP.

Picard‚Äôs Iteration Method (Successive Approximations)

Algorithm (for IVP with $y(0)=0$):

  1. Start with $\phi_0(x) = 0$ (or any function with $\phi_0(0)=0$).
  2. Define recursively:
$$ \phi_{n+1}(x) = \int_0^x f(t, \phi_n(t))\, dt, \quad n = 0,1,2,\dots $$
  1. Under the continuity/boundedness conditions, $\phi_n(x) \to \phi(x)$ (the unique solution).

Key Property:

  • Each $\phi_n$ satisfies the initial condition.
  • Sequence converges to the fixed point of the integral operator.

Detailed Example

IVP: $y' = 2x(1 + y)$, $y(0) = 0$

$f(x,y) = 2x(1+y)$

  • $\phi_0(x) = 0$
  • $\phi_1(x) = \int_0^x 2t(1+0)\,dt = x^2$
  • $\phi_2(x) = \int_0^x 2t(1 + t^2)\,dt = x^2 + \frac{x^4}{2}$
  • $\phi_3(x) = \int_0^x 2t(1 + t^2 + t^4/2)\,dt = x^2 + \frac{x^4}{2} + \frac{x^6}{6}$
  • In general (by induction):
$$ \phi_n(x) = x^2 + \frac{x^4}{2!} + \frac{x^6}{3!} + \cdots + \frac{x^{2n}}{n!} $$

Limit:

$$ \phi(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} \phi_n(x) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{x^{2k}}{k!} = e^{x^2} - 1 $$

Verification:

  • Series converges everywhere (ratio test: $|x^2/(k+1)| \to 0$).
  • Taylor series ‚Üí term-by-term differentiable.
  • Direct substitution confirms it solves both integral equation and original IVP.

Visualization: Successive iterates $\phi_k(x)$ approximate the solution better with increasing $k$, expanding the interval of good approximation.

Proof Sketch: The Limit Solves the IVP

  • Convergence of $\{\phi_n\}$ shown via uniform convergence on compact intervals (under boundedness of $f$).
  • Limit $\phi$ satisfies integral equation (3) by taking limit inside integral (justified by uniform convergence).
  • Hence $\phi$ solves the differential equation.

Uniqueness Proof (via Integral Equation)

Suppose $\phi(x)$ and $\psi(x)$ both solve (3).
Then:

$$ |\phi(x) - \psi(x)| \leq \int_0^{|x|} |f(t,\phi(t)) - f(t,\psi(t))|\, dt $$

By Lipschitz condition (from continuity of $\partial f/\partial y \leq M$ in $D$):

$$ |f(t,\phi) - f(t,\psi)| \leq K |\phi(t) - \psi(t)| $$

Define $u(x) = \int_0^x |\phi(t) - \psi(t)|\, dt$ ($\geq 0$, $u(0)=0$).
Then $u'(x) \leq K u(x)$ on $[0, \alpha/2]$.

Multiply by integrating factor $e^{-Kx}$:

$$ \frac{d}{dx} [e^{-Kx} u(x)] \leq 0 \implies e^{-Kx} u(x) \leq 0 \implies u(x) \leq 0 $$

But $u \geq 0$ ‚Üí $u(x) \equiv 0$ ‚Üí $\phi \equiv \psi$.

Conclusion: Unique solution.

Summary of Lecture 4

  • Existence & Uniqueness proved rigorously via integral equation (3) equivalent to IVP.
  • Picard iteration generates sequence converging to unique solution under standard conditions.
  • Detailed example shows power series solution emerging naturally.
  • Uniqueness via Gronwall-type inequality (integral form).
