Review of Power Series

A power series centered at $x_0$ is

$$ \sum_{m=0}^\infty a_m (x - x_0)^m = a_0 + a_1(x-x_0) + a_2(x-x_0)^2 + \cdots $$
  • $a_m$ are the coefficients.
  • $x_0$ is the center.

The series converges at a point $x$ if the sequence of partial sums

$$ S_N(x) = \sum_{m=0}^N a_m (x-x_0)^m $$

converges (i.e., $\lim_{N\to\infty} S_N(x)$ exists).

  • Always converges at the center $x = x_0$.

Absolute convergence at $x$:

$$ \sum_{m=0}^\infty |a_m| |x-x_0|^m \quad \text{converges} $$

‚Üí implies ordinary convergence.

Radius of Convergence $R$:

  • If the series converges at $x_1 \neq x_0$, it converges absolutely for $|x-x_0| < |x_1-x_0|$.
  • If it diverges at $x_1$, it diverges for $|x-x_0| > |x_1-x_0|$.
  • There exists $R \geq 0$ (or $\infty$) such that the series converges for $|x-x_0| < R$ and diverges for $|x-x_0| > R$.
  • The interval $|x-x_0| < R$ is the interval of convergence (endpoints must be checked separately).

Tests for Radius of Convergence

Root Test:

$$ c = \limsup_{n\to\infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} \quad \Rightarrow \quad R = \frac{1}{c} $$
  • Converges if $c |x-x_0| < 1$, diverges if $>1$.

Ratio Test (often easier):

$$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| \quad \Rightarrow \quad R = \frac{1}{L} $$

(if limit exists).

Examples:

  1. $\sum (-1)^n (x-2)^n$ $R=1$ ‚Üí converges for $1 < x < 3$.
  2. $\sum (-1)^n \frac{x^{2n}}{n^2 2^n}$ $R=\sqrt{2}$ (use ratio or root).

Differentiation & Integration of Power Series

If $\sum a_m (x-x_0)^m = f(x)$ for $|x-x_0| < R$ ($R>0$), then:

  • $f$ is continuous and infinitely differentiable on $|x-x_0| < R$.
  • Term-by-term differentiation/integration is valid inside the radius:
$$ f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n (x-x_0)^{n-1} $$ $$ f''(x) = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1) a_n (x-x_0)^{n-2} $$

(both converge absolutely for $|x-x_0| < R$).

Taylor Series of $f$ about $x_0$:

$$ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(x_0)}{n!} (x-x_0)^n $$
  • $a_n = f^{(n)}(x_0)/n!$.

Analytic function at $x_0$: Can be represented by a power series (Taylor series) with positive radius of convergence around $x_0$.

Examples of analytic functions: $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, polynomials, rational functions (except at poles).

Vanishing coefficients: If $\sum a_m (x-x_0)^m = 0$ for all $x$ in some interval around $x_0$, then $a_m = 0$ for all $m$.

Index Shifting (Useful for ODEs)

It is often convenient to re-index sums so powers align.

Example:

$$ \sum_{n=2}^\infty a_n x^n = \sum_{m=0}^\infty a_{m+2} x^{m+2} $$

(Both contain exactly the same terms.)

Existence of Power Series Solutions

Theorem (Existence of Power Series Solutions):
Consider

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = R(x) $$

If $P(x)$, $Q(x)$, $R(x)$ are analytic at $x=x_0$, then every solution is analytic at $x_0$ and can be represented by a power series in powers of $(x-x_0)$ with positive radius of convergence.

Example: Power Series Solution of $y'' + y = 0$

Assume solution of form

$$ y(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n \quad (\text{centered at } 0) $$

Differentiate:

$$ y' = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n x^{n-1}, \quad y'' = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1) a_n x^{n-2} $$

Plug into ODE:

$$ \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1) a_n x^{n-2} + \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n = 0 $$

Shift index in first sum ($k=n-2$):

$$ \sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+2)(k+1) a_{k+2} x^k + \sum_{k=0}^\infty a_k x^k = 0 $$

Combine:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^\infty \big[(k+2)(k+1) a_{k+2} + a_k\big] x^k = 0 $$

Recurrence:

$$ (k+2)(k+1) a_{k+2} + a_k = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad a_{k+2} = -\frac{a_k}{(k+2)(k+1)} $$

Even and odd coefficients separate:

  • Even: $a_{2k} = \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k)!} a_0$
  • Odd: $a_{2k+1} = \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k+1)!} a_1$

Thus

$$ y(x) = a_0 \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k)!} x^{2k} + a_1 \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k+1)!} x^{2k+1} = a_0 \cos x + a_1 \sin x $$

(Ratio test confirms $R=\infty$).

Summary of Lecture 8

  • Power series: Definition, convergence, radius $R$, interval of convergence.
  • Tests: Root test, ratio test for $R$.
  • Operations: Term-by-term differentiation/integration valid inside radius.
  • Taylor series: Representation of analytic functions.
  • Power series solutions: Exist (and are unique) when $P,Q,R$ analytic ‚Üí solve via recurrence relation on coefficients.
  • Classic example: $y'' + y = 0$ recovers $\cos x$ and $\sin x$ series.

Key Takeaway: Analytic coefficients guarantee analytic solutions expandable as power series ‚Äî the foundation for series solutions of ODEs with variable coefficients.
