Solving ODEs Using Laplace Transform

Three-Step Process:
  1. Transform the ODE (and initial conditions) into an algebraic equation (subsidiary equation).
  2. Solve the algebraic equation by pure algebra.
  3. Invert the solution back to the time domain.
Key Advantages:
  • Solves IVPs directly without finding the general solution first.
  • Handles non-homogeneous terms without solving the homogeneous equation separately.

Definition of Laplace Transform

Let $f(t)$ be defined for $t \geq 0$. The Laplace transform of $f$, denoted $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\}$ or $F(s)$, is $$ \mathcal{L}\{f(t)\}(s) = \int_0^\infty e^{-st} f(t)\, dt $$ whenever the improper integral converges.

Convergence:
  • The integral converges for $s > \alpha$ if $f$ satisfies suitable growth conditions.

Existence Theorem

Theorem: If $f(t)$ is piecewise continuous on $[0,\infty)$ and of exponential order $\alpha$ (i.e., $\exists M>0$ such that $|f(t)| \leq M e^{\alpha t}$ for $t \geq t_0$), then $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\}$ exists for all $s > \alpha$. Proof sketch (using comparison test): Split integral at $t_0$. First part finite (piecewise continuous). Second part bounded by $\int e^{-(s-\alpha)t} M\, dt < \infty$ when $s > \alpha$. Piecewise continuous on $[a,b]$:
  • Finite number of discontinuities.
  • Left and right limits exist and are finite at each discontinuity.
Exponential order examples:
  • Bounded functions ($\alpha=0$).
  • $t^n$ ($\alpha>0$ arbitrary).
  • $\sin \omega t$, $\cos \omega t$ ($\alpha>0$).
  • Counterexample: $e^{t^2}$ (not of exponential order).

First Shifting Theorem

If $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\}(s) = F(s)$ for $s > \alpha$, then for any $a \in \mathbb{R}$, $$ \mathcal{L}\{e^{at} f(t)\}(s) = F(s - a) \quad (s > \alpha + a) $$

Proof: $$ \int_0^\infty e^{-st} e^{at} f(t)\, dt = \int_0^\infty e^{-(s-a)t} f(t)\, dt = F(s-a) $$ Examples:
  • $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\} = 1/(s-a)$
  • $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at} \sin \omega t\} = \omega/[(s-a)^2 + \omega^2]$

Inverse Laplace Transform

$\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F(s)\}(t) = f(t)$ where $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = F(s)$.

Linearity: $$ \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{c_1 F_1(s) + c_2 F_2(s)\} = c_1 \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F_1(s)\} + c_2 \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F_2(s)\} $$ Shifting: $$ \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F(s-a)\}(t) = e^{at} \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F(s)\}(t) $$ Uniqueness (for continuous functions): If $f,g$ are continuous and $\mathcal{L}\{f\} = \mathcal{L}\{g\}$ for $s > \alpha$, then $f(t) = g(t)$ for all $t \geq 0$.

Solving Rational Functions (Partial Fractions / Heaviside)

When $F(s) = P(s)/Q(s)$ with $\deg P < \deg Q$, decompose into partial fractions.

Example: $$ F(s) = \frac{6 + (s+1)(s^2 - 5s + 11)}{(s-1)(s-2)(s-3)} $$ Partial fractions ‚Üí inverse term-by-term.

Summary of Lecture 11

  • Laplace transform converts ODEs/IVPs into algebraic equations.
  • Existence: Piecewise continuous + exponential order ‚Üí transform exists for large $s$.
  • Elementary transforms and first shifting theorem cover most standard functions.
  • Inverse transform: Linear, shifting, partial fractions (Heaviside) for rationals.
  • Uniqueness for continuous functions.
Motivation: Transforms differential equations into algebra ‚Äî ideal for linear constant-coefficient IVPs with discontinuous or exponential forcing.
